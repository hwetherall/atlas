## 1 · Project Goals

**The goal:** Sumitomo aims to build a medical data aggregation and analytics business that generates $5M to $10M per disease-area data source. To achieve this, the company plans to aggregate fragmented Japanese medical records and sell legally compliant insights, joint research, and related services to large U.S. pharmaceutical companies.

**The fundamental question:** What entry structure—joint venture, partnership, direct build, or investment—and commercialization model should Sumitomo use to aggregate Japanese medical data and sell insights to U.S. pharmaceutical companies?

## 2 · Recommendation & Strategic Rationale

**The decision:** Sumitomo must choose between a joint venture, partnership, direct build, or investment to enter the Japanese health data market, and select the specific commercialization model it will use to sell compliant data insights to U.S. pharmaceutical customers.

**Our recommendation:** Per our analysis, we recommend signing a joint venture agreement with NTT Precision Medicine that gives one executive the power to make daily operational decisions, and selling formatted data insights through established U.S. brokers.

**Core rationale**

1. Per our analysis, Japanese privacy laws strictly prohibit sending raw patient records overseas, forcing the venture to use NTT's government certification to process data legally in Japan.
2. Per market research, selling through established U.S. brokers like ConcertAI bypasses the 6-to-12-month enterprise sales cycle and avoids the millions of dollars required to build a direct sales team.
3. Per market research, global competitors like TriNetX and Flatiron Health are signing exclusive agreements with Japanese research hospitals, leaving a closing 12-to-24-month window to secure the data supply.
4. Per benchmark data, successful corporate ventures require the autonomy to make 90 percent of operational decisions independently to avoid stalling daily progress.
5. Per our analysis, the venture must prove external software can translate Japanese clinical notes into the OMOP standard with greater than 95 percent accuracy to maintain profitability.

**Strategic Opportunity.** The external market presents a narrow and contested opportunity. The serviceable obtainable market for selling Japanese oncology data to U.S. pharmaceutical companies is $15M to $30M. Capturing the $5M to $10M revenue target requires winning 16% to 66% of this specific market. Currently, the only demand signal is a single unpaid 900-case pilot proposal from Foundation Medicine, leaving it unproven whether buyers will pay for non-raw data formats. Furthermore, global competitors like TriNetX and Flatiron Health are actively signing exclusive data agreements with top Japanese research hospitals. This creates a 12-to-24-month window to secure the data supply before incumbents lock new entrants out of the market.

**Capability Fit.** The venture lacks the internal readiness required to execute this strategy. The team consists entirely of part-time corporate executives and faces an unresolved joint venture structure that threatens to stall daily operational decisions. The binding gap is the complete reliance on untested external software to translate unstructured Japanese medical records into the required global standard. If this software fails to achieve 95% accuracy, the venture must pay humans to read and type the data, which will drive processing costs above dataset pricing and cause the business to lose money on every sale. Additionally, the venture operates without a formal legal opinion confirming that exporting these data insights complies with Japan's cross-border transfer restrictions. Funding the planned $15M to $20M infrastructure build before proving software accuracy and legal compliance will trap capital in an unviable business.
